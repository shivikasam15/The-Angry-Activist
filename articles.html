Global Warming

What is the greenhouse effect?

The greenhouse effect is the strategic release and containment of heat from the earth. Greenhouse gases such as methane and carbon dioxide are the gases which absorb infrared radiation from the sun. Greenhouse gases are contained in the atmosphere around the earth. Infrared radiation bounces off the surface of the earth and is then absorbed by the greenhouse gases in the atmosphere while some escapes. It is then radiated back towards the Earth’s surface from the atmosphere.

Where does global warming come in?

When there is an increase in greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, more infrared radiation is absorbed heating the atmosphere and the earth even more. This increase in the average temperature is harmful to the environment since entire ecosystems and species survive in specific temperature ranges. Coral reefs, for example are affected by coral bleaching. Corals when healthy, have a symbiotic relationship with zooxanthellae (they rely on each other for their continued survival). The zooxanthellae live in the polyps and is the coral’s primary food source. It produces pigments which give colour to the coral. When the coral becomes stressed due to the temperature, the algae is expelled from the polyps. Due to the coral using its main food source, it turns white.

Buccoo Reef

Buccoo Reef is just off the coast of Tobago, Trinidad and Tobago, and houses 25% of all marine organisms. According to Dr Anjani Ganase’s article, “The Buccoo Reef Crisis: Facts and Fiction” on the blog Wild Tobago, there have been “multiple mass coral bleaching events, disease outbreaks, frequent physical damages and a regime of declining poor water quality.”

Fun Fact: Tobago is home to the world’s largest single brain coral colony measuring 10ft (3m) high and 16ft (5.3m) across. It’s a little way off Speyside and Little Tobago.

According to the World Resources Institute in the article, “Tobago: A Sustainable Future for Buccoo Reef,” the economic contribution associated with Buccoo Reef is estimated at US $7 to $8.8 million per year. The Buccoo Reef also protects a developed section of Tobago’s shore from erosion and storm damage, avoiding an estimated US $5.6 to $10 million per year in damages.

Conservation measures taken:

  • The Marine Area (Preservation & Enhancement) Act of 1970 (in 1973)
  • The Preservation and Enhancement Regulations established legislation for the Buccoo Reef Restricted Area (in 1974).
  • The Institute of Marine Affairs (IMA) prepared a Management Plan on behalf of THA (Tobago House of Assembly) in 1994. However, the only recommendation implemented was the appointment of a park manager.
  • It is in the process of being designated an Environmentally Sensitive Area under the Environmental Management Act (2000) (It was proposed in 2004)
  • The Buccoo Reef is also protected by the Ramsar Convention.

Trinidad and Tobago, A Small Island Developing State

Trinidad & Tobago, A Small Island Developing State

Trinidad and Tobago is one of the 16 Caribbean Small Island Developing States (SIDS). Small Island Developing States are considered to be on the “front lines of climate change.”

While most SIDS rely heavily on fossil fuels, Trinidad and Tobago also produces and exports fossil fuels despite the closure of Petrotrin and the opening of the Heritage Petroleum Company Limited and Paria Fuel Trading Company Limited. However, production has significantly reduced since 1996 as seen by the graph below.

While Small Island Developing States are among the least responsible of all nations for climate change, they will suffer from the effects of climate change. The Caribbean is known to produce some of the most plastic waste in the world which is why Barbados has banned all petrol-based, single-use plastic since April 1st, 2019. While placing a ban may seem simple, they had to offer alternatives first.

This raises the question of what local businesses are doing to support eco-friendly living and how the public is being encouraged to make better decisions.

Massy Stores

Massy Stores, located in Trinidad and Tobago, Barbados, St. Lucia, St Vincent and the Grenadines and Guyana with 48 stores had begun charging for single use plastic bags while a reusable option was made available led to an 80%-90% reduction in the circulation of single use plastic bags which has translated to over 40.5 million bags being removed from circulation between 2018-2019 alone. Additionally, they have begun to change their lighting systems to LED to reduce their energy consumption. They have switched to solar systems in Barbados and St Lucia and have switched to ozone friendly fuel for their air conditioners and refrigerators. They have also partnered with environmental agencies across the region.

The Carbon Zero Initiative of Trinidad & Tobago (CZITT)

The Carbon Zero Initiative of Trinidad & Tobago (CZITT) is a non-profit organisation involved in awareness, advocacy, programmes and projects geared towards the reducing and offsetting of greenhouse gas emissions. Some of their projects include the Climate Quiz, Project Zero and 1000 Trees Challenge. In the National Secondary Schools’ Climate Quiz, 112 schools in Trinidad and Tobago. The top 16 schools were then invited to develop a project which fights against climate change and create a video on the project. Five schools took up the challenge with ASJA Girls’ College San Fernando placing first, Naparima Girls’ High School placing second and Naparima College placing third. In Project Zero, St Augustine Girls’ High School and Presentation College attempted to make their schools carbon neutral. Finally, in their 1000 trees challenge, CZITT partnered with schools, clubs, corporations to plant and record trees. They mainly planted moringa trees and are developing a local moringa industry for export.

Environmental Management Authority’s (EMA) iCARE Project

The Environmental Management Authority was formed by the Government of the Republic of Trinidad and Tobago’s Ministry of Planning and Development. They introduced The Recyclable Solid Waste Collection Project (RSWC) in 2015 as a successor to Beverage Container Collection Project. It was then branded as the iCARE Program through sponsorship by the Green Fund. Part of the iCARE Program is public education where it encourages citizens to separate their beverage containers and drop it off in one of their iCARE Collection Bins in a clear or frosted bag. ICARE has established over 80 collection bins nationwide.

The Last Straw and More

The Last Straw and More is a local (Trinidad and Tobago) company owned by Sangeeta Praimsingh. Plastic straws are part of the plastic problem, it is projected that by 2050, 99% of all sea bird species will have ingested plastic. Plastic straws, like most plastic waste, end up in the oceans due to littering, improper disposal etc. The company manufactures and sells hand-made, locally produced bamboo straws among other eco-friendly, reusable items. The straws are made from bamboo grown in Trinidad and are hand-made. They are distributed at Praimsingh’s in Curepe and the Santa Cruz Green Market. They can be contacted through their Facebook page, The Last Straw & More TT. They use their platform to educate by posting information on the negative impact that single-use straws can have on the environment.

The5R's

The 5 R’s

We are all used to hearing Reduce, Reuse and Recycle but there are 5R’s in sustainability, and they are:

  1. Refuse- Saying NO to single use plastics and products that use excessive plastic wrapping. 
  2. Reduce- Buys goods that will last longer and cut back on your purchasing of short-span items and items that you don’t have any intention of using.
  3. Reuse- Replace single-use items that can be used several times. Where possible, instead of tossing out, find a way to repurpose items.
  4. Recycle- After the first 3 steps, you recycle your remaining plastics, glass, paper and metal.
  5. Rot- Kitchen scraps can be left to rot or compost.

Many recyclables are made of non-renewable resources such as fossil fuels and natural gas. Thus, it is imperative that we try to conserve these resources through the reduction of use and the recycling of that which was already used. For instance, fossil fuels are used to make plastics. Additionally, Carbon Dioxide is a by-product of fossil fuel combustion. In the recycling process, recyclables are sorted if necessary, crushed and/or shredded, washed and made into pellets which can then be used to make new items. This process helps reduce landfill content, reduce the amount of recyclables being produced being produced and keeps most of it out of waterways.
